Complete Filming Setup Guide

Everything you need to know to film professional, credible content — from setup to delivery.

How to Film Like a Professional

You don't need a full production team to look credible on camera — you just need to know what actually matters. We walk you through exactly how we set up every client's filming space: camera positioning, lighting, microphone placement, and the small details that separate amateur footage from content that builds real trust with your audience.

Whether you're filming with a smartphone or a professional camera, this guide gives you everything you need to show up looking and sounding like the credible, established business owner you are.

Before You Film — The Room

Choose your space

Pick a quiet room, ideally away from street noise, fans, or air conditioning units running nearby. A smaller room with soft furnishings (carpet, curtains, a couch) sounds better than a large, empty, echo-heavy space.

Set your background

Keep it clean and intentional — either a solid wall with brand-colored accent lighting behind you, or a lightly styled area (a bookshelf, plant, or simple decor) that stays out of focus. Avoid busy, cluttered, or distracting backgrounds.

Camera & Tripod

Positioning

Set your camera at eye level or very slightly above — never below, as this creates an unflattering upward angle. Position yourself far enough from the camera that your face doesn't distort (roughly 5-7 feet is ideal for most lenses).

Tripod stability

Use a sturdy tripod — a wobbly or cheap tripod is one of the fastest ways to make footage look unprofessional. If you don't have one yet, even a stable stack of books or a shelf can work temporarily, but a proper tripod should be an early investment.

Framing

Center yourself in the frame with a small amount of headroom above your head — not too much empty space, not too tight. A chest-up or waist-up shot is standard for talking-head content.

Settings

Shoot in the highest resolution your camera supports — 4K if possible. This gives your editor room to crop in for tighter framing without losing quality.

Lighting

Key light (your main light)

Place your primary light slightly to one side of the camera, roughly 45 degrees from your face, angled slightly downward. This is what lights your face and gives you a clean, professional look.

Fill light (optional but recommended)

A second, softer light on the opposite side of your key light — this softens shadows on the other side of your face for a more even, polished look.

Avoid

Never film with a bright window or light source directly behind you — this creates a silhouette effect where your face becomes dark and hard to see. If you must film near a window, face toward it, not away from it.

Consistency

Use the same lighting setup every time you film. Consistency across videos makes your channel look more professional and intentional, even if the setup is simple.

Microphone & Audio

Use an external microphone whenever possible

Built-in camera or laptop microphones pick up excessive room echo and background noise. Even an affordable lapel or USB microphone will sound dramatically better than a built-in one.

Positioning

If using a lapel mic, clip it 6-8 inches below your chin, centered on your chest. If using a stand-mounted microphone, keep it just outside the camera frame, angled toward your mouth.

Reduce background noise

Turn off fans, air conditioning, and anything with a hum or vibration before you start recording. Close windows if there's outside noise.

Record a few seconds of silence first

Before you start talking, let the camera roll on silence for 5-10 seconds. This gives our editing team a clean noise sample to use for background noise removal.

During the Recording

Don't stop for small mistakes

If you stumble on a line, pause for a second, take a breath, and simply repeat the sentence. Our editors will cut around it — there's no need to restart the entire recording.

Energy and pacing

Speak at a calm, confident pace — slightly slower than normal conversation. Let your sentences breathe. This reads as more credible and mature on camera than rushed, high-energy delivery.

Look at the camera lens, not the screen

This is what creates the feeling of eye contact with your viewer. It takes a little practice if you're not used to it, but it makes a significant difference in how connected your audience feels to you.

After You Film

Label your files clearly

Name your raw footage files with the date and topic (e.g., "Video3_Title_Date") before uploading, so our team can organize everything quickly.

Upload promptly

The sooner we receive your raw footage, the sooner your content goes into production. Upload directly to your shared Google Drive folder as soon as you're done filming.

Quick Checklist Before You Hit Record

  • Room is quiet, background is clean
  • Camera is stable, framed correctly, and set to the highest resolution
  • Key light (and fill light, if available) is positioned correctly
  • Microphone is connected, positioned, and tested
  • A few seconds of silence recorded first
  • You're centered, eye level with the camera, ready to go
